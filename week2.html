Week 2: Economic Mobility, Equity & Systems Thinking

👋 Welcome to Week 2

In Week 1, you explored workforce development as a system.

In Week 2, we go deeper.

This session shifts the focus from who is in the system → to how the system produces outcomes.

Across communities, we see the same pattern:

  • some individuals advance
  • others remain stuck
  • and many cycle through services without long-term mobility

This is not random.

👉 These outcomes are shaped by how systems are designed.

Why This Matters

Economic mobility is often framed as an individual responsibility.

But in reality, mobility is influenced by:

  • access to education and training
  • labor market structure and job quality
  • employer hiring practices
  • availability of supports (transportation, childcare, housing)
  • policy and funding decisions
👉 When systems are misaligned, even strong programs struggle to produce lasting outcomes.
Week 2 Focus:

Understanding economic mobility as a system outcome — and identifying where workforce systems either enable or limit advancement.

📊 The Opportunity Accelerator Framework

The Opportunity Accelerator (OA) provides a structure for responding to these challenges.

It organizes workforce practice into three phases:

  • Assess & Plan → Understanding needs and system conditions
  • Equip & Connect → Delivering services and building pathways
  • Launch & Evaluate → Supporting outcomes and measuring success

Applying OA in Week 2

This week, the OA framework becomes more than a model.

👉 It becomes a lens for analyzing system performance.

You will begin asking:

  • Where are outcomes breaking down?
  • Which Service Delivery Areas influence those breakdowns?

🔗 Understanding Economic Mobility as a System

Economic mobility is not a single event.

It is a process shaped by multiple systems interacting over time.

Key System Dynamics

Structural Inequality

Access to opportunity is not evenly distributed. Historical and structural factors shape who benefits from workforce systems.

Labor Market Segmentation

Not all jobs provide the same level of stability, wages, or advancement opportunity.

Some participants are funneled into:

  • low-wage roles
  • limited advancement pathways
  • unstable employment

Racialized Workforce Outcomes

Workforce outcomes often differ across populations due to structural inequities, not individual effort.

System Design

Programs, policies, and partnerships influence:

  • who enters services
  • what services they receive
  • what outcomes are possible
👉 Economic mobility is produced by systems — not just individual effort.

🧠 Systems Thinking in Workforce Development

Systems thinking helps us move beyond symptoms to underlying causes.

Instead of asking:

❌ “Why aren’t participants succeeding?”

We ask:

✅ “What conditions are shaping these outcomes?”

Core Systems Thinking Practices

  • identifying patterns across participants
  • mapping relationships between barriers
  • examining how systems interact
  • identifying leverage points for change

Root Cause vs Surface-Level Thinking

Surface-Level:

  • low completion rates
  • low placement rates
  • low retention

Root Causes Might Include:

  • misaligned training programs
  • lack of employer demand
  • inadequate supports
  • structural barriers

👔 Systems Leadership in Economic Mobility

Systems leadership requires:

  • seeing beyond individual programs
  • understanding system interactions
  • making decisions based on evidence and patterns
  • aligning resources across partners
  • addressing root causes — not just symptoms
👉 Leadership is not about managing services.

It is about shaping how systems produce outcomes.

🟦 Stepping Into Your Role

As a workforce systems leader, your role is to:

🔍 Applied Activity: Systems Mapping

Purpose

To analyze how workforce systems produce outcomes and identify how OA Service Delivery Areas influence those outcomes.

Instructions

Using your ecosystem map from Week 1, build a systems map that connects:

Step 1 — Participant Outcomes

  • job placement
  • retention
  • wage progression
  • credential attainment
  • advancement

Step 2 — Barriers

  • transportation
  • childcare
  • skills gaps
  • employer practices
  • program design limitations

Step 3 — OA Service Delivery Areas

  • Which SDA influences this issue?
  • Is the issue due to design, delivery, or alignment?
👉 This activity shifts focus from “what is happening” to “why it is happening.”
